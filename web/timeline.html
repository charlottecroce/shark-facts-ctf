A Shark Timeline

Sharks are one of the oldest surviving groups of vertebrates on Earth. Their fossil record stretches back further than dinosaurs, trees, and even insects, and they have survived all five of the planet's great mass extinction events.

~450 million years ago

The earliest fossil evidence for sharks or their close ancestors appears in the Late Ordovician: a handful of tiny scales. Sharks were already ancient long before the first dinosaurs showed up, roughly 230 million years ago.

~380 million years ago

Cladoselache swims the Devonian seas. At one to two metres long, with two dorsal fins, paired fins and a forked tail, it is unmistakably shark-shaped and one of the earliest sharks known from more complete fossils.

~359 million years ago

The Carboniferous ushers in what is sometimes called the "Golden Age of Sharks," a stretch when the group diversified dramatically in size and shape.

~66 million years ago

The asteroid impact that ends the age of the non-avian dinosaurs also wipes out many of the largest sharks. Smaller, deep-water, fish-eating species survive and the lineage carries on.

~23 to 3.6 million years ago

Otodus megalodon, the megalodon, rules the seas as the largest macropredatory shark ever known, reaching an estimated 15 to 18 metres. Despite the myth, it was not a direct ancestor of the great white. Hammerheads, thought to be the youngest shark group, also appear around this time.

- Natural History Museum
